WINE FOOD PAIRING GUIDE
========================

HIGH QUALITY RED WINE PAIRINGS (Score 7-8)
A high-quality, full-bodied red wine pairs excellently with grilled red meats such as ribeye steak, lamb chops, and venison. The tannins in quality red wine cut through the fat of red meats beautifully. Aged cheeses like parmesan, aged cheddar, and manchego complement the complexity of high-scoring wines. Mushroom-based dishes including risotto with porcini and beef bourguignon are classic pairings. Dark chocolate with at least 70% cocoa pairs well with robust reds. Roasted vegetables with herbs like rosemary and thyme enhance the earthy notes in quality red wine.

MEDIUM QUALITY RED WINE PAIRINGS (Score 5-6)
Medium quality red wines pair well with pasta dishes featuring tomato-based sauces, pizza, burgers, and grilled chicken. These wines work well with mild to medium cheeses. Charcuterie boards with salami, prosciutto, and olives are excellent companions. BBQ dishes with smoky, tangy sauces match the fruitiness of medium-quality reds. Hearty soups and stews benefit from the structure of medium-quality red wine.

LOW QUALITY RED WINE PAIRINGS (Score 3-4)
Lower quality wines with higher volatile acidity or off-flavors work better in cooking than as drinking wines. Use them in marinades for meats. They can be used in wine-based sauces for pasta. Mulled wine recipes benefit from lower quality wines since spices mask defects. Sangria made with fruit and spirits can make lower quality wines enjoyable.

PAIRING BY CHEMICAL PROPERTIES

High Alcohol Wine (above 13%): Pairs with rich, fatty foods. Avoid delicate fish and light salads which get overwhelmed. Good with aged cheeses, red meats, and hearty stews.

High Acidity Wine (pH below 3.3): Works beautifully with oily fish, creamy dishes, and foods with natural fat content. The acidity cuts through richness. Good with salmon, cream sauces, and butter-based dishes.

High Tannin Wine (often indicated by lower pH and higher sulphates): Pair with protein-rich foods. Tannins bind to proteins, making the wine taste smoother. Red meat is the classic pairing.

High Sulphate Wine: Generally indicates a wine with more preservative character. These wines pair well with cured meats and charcuterie.

Sweet Wine (higher residual sugar): Pair with equally sweet or salty foods. Blue cheese and sweet wine is a classic combination. Avoid with dry, savory main courses where the sweetness creates imbalance.

WINE AND CHEESE PAIRING GUIDE
Cabernet Sauvignon style wines: Aged cheddar, gouda, manchego
Syrah/Shiraz style wines: Aged gouda, gruyere, blue cheese
Merlot style wines: Brie, camembert, mild cheddar
Pinot Noir style wines: Mild goat cheese, brie, gruyere
Sangiovese/Chianti style wines: Parmesan, pecorino, asiago

WINE SERVICE TEMPERATURE
Quality red wines (score 7-8): Serve at 16-18°C (60-64°F)
Medium quality reds (score 5-6): Serve at 15-17°C (59-62°F)
Light reds: Serve slightly cooler at 13-15°C (55-59°F)
Chilling red wine slightly below room temperature enhances freshness and suppresses alcohol harshness.

DECANTING RECOMMENDATIONS
Young full-bodied reds (high alcohol, high tannin): Decant for 1-2 hours before serving. This allows oxidation to soften tannins and open up aromas.
Medium-bodied reds: 30 minutes of decanting is usually sufficient.
Older vintage wines: Decant gently and serve quickly as they may fade after prolonged air exposure.
